Photos are nice. I still remember film and video tapes from my early childhood. We owned many video cameras and point-and-shoot and compacts. I remember using one (and losing one, unfortunately), but I wasn't really walking around taking pictures like I am now. This is because I was 7.

A bit later, I got into smartphone. I had a camera that's always on me, so I started taking pictures. At first, of the people around me engaging in various activities. But then people failed me, so I started to shoot nature instead. Moving in Canada means I had a lot of opportunity to explore nature. For example, I've taken great photos of sunsets at Muskoka.

I once scoffed at the idea of owning a dedicated camera. It was hard to imagine that it could take better photos and be as easy to use as my phone. Back then, DSLRs were mainstream, and mirrorless cameras were only begin to popularize. DSLRs are heavy and clunky. I used one during a media class in high school. Wasn't a fan of the camera at all, but this planted a seed for the interest in photography.

My first experience with a real camera is due to my grandfather (on my mom's side). At the very young age of 70, he specializes in whimsical activies such as travelling around the country, visiting scenic locations (tourist attractions, parks, reservers, etc.), and taking photos of everything, along with a large group of travel friends. All of them are retired, and none has as high of a stroke-to-chronic-diseases ratio as him. He gifted me a high-end compact, the LX10, after hearing that I had developed an interest. The LX10 is a nifty and capable little camera which I underestimated at first. I've had it for a while and never took it out, but one day I suddenly feel motivated to take it out for a shoot. I had a very nice day with it.

The rabbit hole demands itself to be dug deeper. During my brief and wonderful 2025 summer in Toronto, I acquired the X-S20 with a kit lens. I would then proceed to carry it with me everyday, everywhere it goes, and take photos of everything I found to be nice. (This was not ideal, as I'll elaborate later.) I love the camera and still use it everyday. Hopefully I will have it for a long time, before GAS (Gear Acquisition Syndrome) finally gets to me.
