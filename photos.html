Photos are nice. I still remember the film and video tape era from my early childhood. We owned many video cameras and point-and-shoot cameras. I remember using one (and losing one, unfortunately), but I wasn't really walking around taking pictures like I am now. This is because I was 7.

A bit later, I got into smartphones. I had a camera that's always on me, so I started taking pictures. At first, of the people around me engaging in various activities. But then people failed me, so I started to shoot nature instead. Moving in Canada means I had a lot of opportunity to explore nature. For example, I've taken great photos of sunsets at Muskoka.

I once scoffed at the idea of owning a dedicated camera. It was hard to imagine that it could take better photos and be as easy to use as my phone. Back then, DSLRs were mainstream, and mirrorless cameras were only begin to popularize. DSLRs are heavy and clunky. I used one during a media class in high school. Wasn't a fan of the camera at all, but this planted a seed for the interest in photography.

My first experience with a real camera is due to my grandfather (on my mom's side). At the very young age of 70, he specializes in whimsical activies such as travelling around the country, visiting scenic locations (tourist attractions, parks, reservers, etc.), and taking photos of everything, along with a large group of travel friends. All of them are retired, and none has as high of a stroke-to-chronic-diseases ratio as him. He gifted me a high-end compact, the LX10, after hearing that I had developed an interest. The LX10 is a nifty and capable little camera which I underestimated at first. I've had it for a while and never took it out, but one day I suddenly feel motivated to take it out for a shoot. I had a very nice day with it, and from that day I start to shoot more and more.

The rabbit hole demands itself to be dug deeper. During my brief and wonderful 2025 summer in Toronto, I acquired the X-S20 with a kit lens. I would then proceed to carry it with me everyday, everywhere it goes, and take photos of everything I found to be nice. I got to try a few lenses out as well, and got into an argument with a store owner because I keep buying and returning them. (This was not ideal, as I'll elaborate later.) I love the camera and still use it everyday. Hopefully I will have it for a long time, before GAS (Gear Acquisition Syndrome) finally gets to me.

So how does one take photos? I'll say a few things based on my experience. Bear in mind, I'm still only an enthusiast at photography, but I believe these tips will be helpful nonetheless.

What camera to get

Many say the best camera is the one you have with you. I find that to be false. The best camera is the one you have with you and you enjoy using. If you like taking pictures on your phone, and you like the images coming out of it, just use your phone! Nowadays the difference in image quality between a phone and a camera is smaller than ever before. Plus, it really saves your wallet from deflation.

If you found your phone to be inadequate, though, a camera might be worth it. This could be the case even if the camera does not provide a noticeable upgrade in image quality, but simply a better user experience. Just because I can open a bottle of a beer with my teeth doesn't mean I won't benefit a great deal from owning a bottle opener!

Whatever camera you get, keep in mind the most important thing it should have: you should like using it. What great things other say about a camera don't matter even a tiny bit if you don't enjoy holding it in your hand(s?) and shooting with it. When you like the tool you're using, it is so much easier and more fun to create photos you'll love.

Here are the most common categories of cameras, and whether or not you should get one of them:

  • Interchangeable-lens Mirrorless. This is the most common one and what professionals use. You get a wide selection of models and lenses to use with it. They are usually built to last a very long time.
  • Fixed-lens Mirrorless. Also somewhat popular. You miss the ability to change lenses, but the cameras are usually smaller and lighter, and as single packages, provide a more uniform and refined experience.
  • DSLR. These are heavier, bigger, older. You get optical viewfinder (through the reflex mirror) which you might prefer over the electronic viewfinder or LCD screen of the mirrorless ones. I certainly do. Due to their age, their image quality might not be as good as the mirrorless, but you get relatively cheaper lens.
  • Point-and-shoot. These are cameras that uses smaller sensors and lenses, doesn't require (or only minimal) focusing, and usually have a nostalgic look, reminiscent of early smartphones. These are cheap, portable, easy to use and fun.

None of these are bad choices. Choose whatever that's in your budget and sounds apppealing. Be careful — if you opt for the first option, you will constantly thinking about better gear, even if what you currently have works absolutely fine. (Speaking from experience.)

I didn't include any film cameras because in this day and age they are no longer economically viable, especially as a hobby. Buying a roll of film costs around $20. Developing and scanning costs around that much as well, unless you do it yourself. That said, I will include some brands selling film cameras with extra fun factor.

Here's a list of camera manufacturers. The big 3: Canon, Nikon, and Sony. The smaller 4 (?): Fujifilm, OM systems, Panasonic. The unique ones: Hasselblad, Leica, Ricoh/Pentax, and Sigma. Fun film ones: Contax, Lomography, Minolta, Rollei, etc..
